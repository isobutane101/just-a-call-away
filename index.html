Why We Started This

The story behind Just a Call Away

"In emergencies across the United States, many people instinctively dial 911 for help. However, in communities where English isn't the primary language, this critical emergency number isn't always the first call residents make."

— The Seattle Times, March 2026

That single sentence, from the Seattle Times feature on our work, captures something we kept noticing in our own community here on the Eastside. When we asked elderly aunties, uncles, and grandparents what they would do if a loved one collapsed at home, the answers we heard most often weren't "call 911." They were "call my son," "drive to the hospital," or "wait and see."

The instinct in those moments isn't wrong — it's human. But it costs minutes that heart attack and stroke patients can't afford to lose. We wanted to understand why 911 wasn't the first call, and what we found wasn't one big problem. It was a stack of smaller ones.

The barriers we kept hearing about

  • Language. Many of our elders speak English well in everyday life, but a panicked emergency call is different. Not knowing that 911 offers free interpreters in 140+ languages means people hesitate, or hand the phone to a relative who isn't there.
  • Fear of authorities. In immigrant communities, a call to a government number can feel risky. People worry about being asked their immigration status, about ICE, about anything that might put a family member at risk. 911 doesn't ask, and won't — but no one ever told them that.
  • Uncertainty about what counts. Is chest pain "serious enough"? Is grandma's slurred speech a stroke or just exhaustion? Without a clear list of warning signs in their own language, people second-guess themselves until it's too late.
  • Unfamiliarity with the system. What happens after you dial? Will paramedics break down the door? Will there be a bill? Will the neighbors see? The unknown is its own deterrent.

Why we picked this up

We're two brothers who grew up in this community. The people the Seattle Times article describes aren't abstract to us — they're our neighbors, the families at our temple, the grandparents we see at the grocery store on weekends. The gap the article points to is one we've watched close up, and it's one we're in a position to help close.

So we built the simplest, most direct thing we could: free, printable flyers in English, Hindi, Gujarati, Marathi, Tamil, and Telugu that lay out exactly when to call 911, what to say, and what to expect. No jargon. No fear. Just the information our families wish someone had handed them years ago.

That's the start. The flyers are getting hung up in temples, libraries, and grocery stores across the Eastside. Workshops are next. And we're working with King County to bring this to even more communities.

Follow our work on Instagram @justacallawayofficial — that's where we share new flyers, distribution updates, and stories from the families we meet.

Why This Matters

Every year, immigrant families lose loved ones because they didn't know when or how to call 911.

Language barriers. Fear of authorities. Unfamiliarity with the system. These obstacles cost precious minutes when seconds matter most.

We're changing that. Our free, culturally-sensitive guides give families the knowledge they need to act fast in emergencies. Because no one should lose a parent, grandparent, or loved one due to a preventable gap in awareness.

We are currently working with King County officials to expand the reach and impact of our initiative, bringing emergency preparedness resources to even more communities across the region.
